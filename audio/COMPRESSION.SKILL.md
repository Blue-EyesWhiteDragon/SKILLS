## Circuit Topology Reference

> Research phase completed prior to genre sections. All genre circuit assignments trace back to the characteristics documented here.

### FET (Field Effect Transistor)
- **Detection Method:** Peak; feed-back topology — sidechain signal derived post gain-reduction stage
- **Transient Response:** Attack range 20μs–800μs; release range 50ms–1.1s. Because the sidechain reacts to an already-compressed signal, the compressor is self-limiting in overshoot. The "All Buttons In" configuration (all ratio buttons simultaneously engaged) shifts the FET bias point, producing a ratio of approximately 12:1–20:1 with a plateau-shaped compression curve rather than a smooth slope, increased Total Harmonic Distortion (THD), and a lag on initial transients before heavy gain reduction sets in. [CB]
- **Harmonic Distortion Profile:** Even-order harmonics generated by transformer coupling at input/output stages and by FET non-linearity under drive. THD increases with input level. At extreme input drive, distortion is audible as saturation prior to gain reduction engaging. [CB]
- **Known Limitations:** Colors signal even with compression bypassed; raises noise floor at high makeup gain settings; fixed-ceiling threshold on hardware implementations (e.g., UREI 1176) — threshold is set indirectly via input drive rather than a dedicated control; ratio and threshold interact non-linearly. [CB]

### VCA (Voltage Controlled Amplifier)
- **Detection Method:** Peak; topology is design-dependent — feed-forward (sidechain reads pre-compression signal) or feed-back (sidechain reads post-compression signal). Feed-forward VCAs respond more predictably and instantaneously to transient peaks; feed-back VCAs produce smoother, less over-compressed gain reduction. [CB]
- **Transient Response:** Very fast attack and release times; highly precise and repeatable. Control voltage–to–gain relationship is linear in dB, making time constant behavior consistent across different input levels. [CB]
- **Harmonic Distortion Profile:** Very low THD; VCA designs are characteristically clean. Integrated VCA chips offer low output impedance and high bandwidth with predictable gain control. [CB]
- **Known Limitations:** Can produce a clinical or harsh character at extreme gain reduction settings without analog circuit coloration; feed-forward designs can over-compress on instantaneous transient peaks if attack is set below 1ms without look-ahead. [CB]

### Opto (Optical)
- **Detection Method:** Program-dependent; LDR (Light Dependent Resistor) attenuates signal in proportion to lamp brightness, which is driven by the sidechain. Attack and release are therefore governed by the physical response time of the lamp and photocell assembly rather than a control voltage. [CB]
- **Transient Response:** Inherently slow and non-linear. Two-stage release curve: a fast initial recovery (approximately 60ms for the first 50% of release), followed by a slow settling tail that can extend over several seconds. Photocell "memory" effect — sustained heavy compression above threshold causes progressively longer release times on subsequent cycles. Attack time is frequency-dependent: lower frequencies produce slower attack because the lamp ramp-up is proportional to signal energy. [CB]
- **Harmonic Distortion Profile:** Minimal. Harmonic content is primarily a function of any transformer stages present in the circuit, not the optical attenuator itself. [CB]
- **Known Limitations:** No precise time constant control in classic implementations (e.g., LA-2A); program-dependency makes behavior difficult to predict on rapidly changing dynamics; photocell memory effect creates inconsistent release behavior under sustained heavy program material. [CB]

### Vari-Mu (Variable Mu / Tube)
- **Detection Method:** Feed-back; RMS-like. Gain reduction is produced by varying the bias voltage of a tube stage, causing the tube's amplification factor (mu) to decrease as signal level increases. [CB]
- **Transient Response:** Slowest of all topologies. Attack and release are both program-dependent, with release times in the range of hundreds of milliseconds to multiple seconds. Ratio is not fixed — it rises progressively with input level, producing an inherently soft-knee compression curve. [CB]
- **Harmonic Distortion Profile:** Even-order harmonics generated by the tube stage; adds a low-level saturation characteristic to all signals passing through the circuit, including signals below the compression threshold. [CB]
- **Known Limitations:** Unsuitable for fast transient control; no fixed ratio available; tube bias drift requires periodic calibration; slowest attack of any topology makes it ineffective for peak catching. [CB]

### Digital (Feed-forward / Look-ahead)
- **Detection Method:** Peak or RMS; feed-forward with look-ahead buffer. The look-ahead buffer introduces a short delay (typically 0.1ms–5ms) on the main signal path, allowing the sidechain to compute gain reduction in advance of the incoming transient. [CB]
- **Transient Response:** Can achieve true 0ms attack by pre-reading the incoming signal via the look-ahead buffer. Fully configurable time constants with no circuit-imposed lower limit. [CB]
- **Harmonic Distortion Profile:** None inherent. Digital compression is arithmetically precise; any harmonic coloration is the result of deliberate analog circuit modeling, not the gain reduction process itself. [CB]
- **Known Limitations:** Look-ahead buffer introduces latency proportional to the look-ahead time; at extreme settings (instantaneous attack, very short release) without analog modeling, gain reduction can sound harsh or produce inter-sample peaks on output; phase response may be affected by buffer implementation. [CB]

## General

### Attack

**Definition:** The time elapsed between the input signal exceeding the threshold and the compressor reaching its full gain reduction ratio. In technical terms, it is the slope of the gain reduction envelope from unity gain to the set ratio.

**Signal-Domain Behaviour:** Attack time determines what portion of the transient envelope passes through the gain reduction stage unaffected. At attack times below 1ms, the compressor engages before the transient peak has fully propagated, attenuating the crest of the waveform and reducing the crest factor of the signal. At attack times above 10ms, the initial attack transient passes the gain reduction stage at or near unity gain, preserving the peak-to-RMS relationship of the source material. The relationship between attack time and signal frequency is critical: a 10ms attack on a 50Hz signal (20ms period) will engage mid-cycle, introducing low-frequency distortion artifacts.

**Common Misapplication:** Setting attack below 5ms on percussive material or vocals, which attenuates the consonant transient before the human auditory system has resolved it, resulting in a reduction in perceived intelligibility and attack definition. The error is frequently identified as a "muffled" or "pushed back" quality in the mixed signal.

---

### Release

**Definition:** The time elapsed between the input signal falling below the threshold and the compressor returning to unity gain (zero gain reduction).

**Signal-Domain Behaviour:** Release time determines the rate at which the gain reduction envelope returns to zero. Release times that are too short relative to the tempo of the material cause the gain reduction stage to re-engage at the onset of the next transient before the previous gain reduction cycle has fully resolved, producing an audible and rhythmically unintentional amplitude modulation artifact (commonly called "pumping"). Conversely, release times that are too long cause residual gain reduction to persist across multiple signal events, reducing the dynamic contrast between successive transients and raising the average RMS level of the signal without a corresponding reduction in peaks.

**Common Misapplication:** Setting release shorter than the period of the lowest frequency component in the signal (e.g., <20ms on a 50Hz bass signal with a 20ms period) causes the gain reduction circuit to re-trigger within a single waveform cycle, producing intra-cycle amplitude modulation that manifests as harmonic distortion, particularly audible as "chattering" or "buzzing" on low-frequency content.

---

### Threshold

**Definition:** The amplitude level at which the compressor begins to apply gain reduction. Signals below the threshold pass through the gain reduction stage at unity gain; signals above the threshold are attenuated according to the set ratio.

**Signal-Domain Behaviour:** Threshold determines the crossover point between the unprocessed and compressed regions of the signal's dynamic range. The position of the threshold relative to the signal's RMS floor and peak ceiling determines the amount of time the compressor spends in gain reduction (duty cycle). A threshold set at the signal's RMS floor will compress both the body and the peaks of the signal. A threshold set above the RMS floor but below the peak ceiling will compress only the uppermost dynamic peaks, leaving the signal body unaffected.

**Common Misapplication:** Setting threshold at a fixed dBFS value without first measuring the RMS floor of the source material. Because different signals have different gain-staging characteristics, an absolute dBFS threshold value has no consistent relationship to the signal's dynamic structure across different sources or sessions.

---

### Makeup Gain

**Definition:** A secondary gain stage applied after the gain reduction circuit, used to restore the peak or RMS level of the compressed signal to a target operating level. Makeup gain compensates for the amplitude loss introduced by compression.

**Signal-Domain Behaviour:** Because compression is a subtractive process (gain reduction reduces peak amplitude), the output signal of a compressor will have a lower peak level than the input signal. Makeup gain applies a fixed positive gain offset to the compressed signal to restore it to the pre-compression peak level or to a desired RMS target. This effectively raises the RMS level of the signal relative to its peaks, which is the primary mechanism by which compression increases perceived loudness. The amount of makeup gain required is approximately equal to the amount of gain reduction applied at the highest peak of the signal.

**Common Misapplication:** Using makeup gain to compare the compressed signal to the dry signal at matched loudness is frequently omitted, causing the compressed signal to appear "better" simply due to its higher RMS level. A/B comparisons between compressed and dry signal must be conducted at matched RMS levels to evaluate the compression processing independently of loudness.

---

### RMS

**Definition:** Root Mean Square. A mathematical measure of the average power of an audio signal, calculated as the square root of the mean of the squared amplitude values over a defined integration window.

**Signal-Domain Behaviour:** RMS detection responds to the sustained energy of the signal rather than its instantaneous peak amplitude. Because the human auditory system perceives loudness as a function of sustained energy (not instantaneous peaks), RMS-based compression more closely tracks perceived loudness than peak detection. An RMS detector applies gain reduction that is proportional to the average power of the signal over the integration window, producing smoother and more gradual gain reduction than peak detection on transient-rich material.

**Common Misapplication:** Confusing RMS detection with slower attack times. RMS detection is a property of the sidechain's measurement method (how the level is computed); attack time is a property of the gain reduction envelope (how fast the compressor reacts once the threshold is crossed). A compressor can have a fast attack time and an RMS detection circuit simultaneously.

---

### RMS Window (FL Studio Compressors)

**Definition:** The integration time, measured in milliseconds, used by the sidechain's RMS detection circuit to calculate the average power of the incoming signal. Specific to FL Studio's compressor implementations (e.g., Fruity Peak Controller, Parametric Compressor).

**Signal-Domain Behaviour:** A short RMS window (e.g., 1ms–5ms) increases the detector's sensitivity to rapid amplitude changes, moving the detection behaviour closer to peak detection. A long RMS window (e.g., 50ms–200ms) increases the averaging period, causing the detector to respond to the overall signal body and ignore short transient excursions. In practice, a short window with a fast attack produces behaviour equivalent to a fast-responding peak compressor, while a long window with a slower attack produces behaviour equivalent to a leveling amplifier or slow RMS bus compressor.

**Common Misapplication:** Setting the RMS window longer than the release time, which causes the detector to still be averaging a high signal level while the gain reduction envelope has already returned to unity, producing incorrect threshold-crossing detection and erratic gain reduction behaviour.

## Vocal

### Pop

**Circuit:** FET (stage 1, peak catching) in series with Opto (stage 2, leveling) — serial configuration [CB]
**Detection Method:** FET: Peak, feed-back; Opto: Program-dependent [CB]
**Attack:** FET: 200μs–800μs; Opto: Program-dependent (governed by lamp ramp-up rate) [CB, GA]
**Release:** FET: 50ms–100ms; Opto: Program-dependent, two-stage (initial ~60ms, tail 500ms–2s) [CB, GA]
**Ratio:** FET: 4:1–8:1 (peak catching); Opto: 2:1–3:1 (leveling) [GA]
**Threshold:** FET stage set to intersect the peak ceiling of the signal, targeting only transient excursions above the RMS floor; Opto stage set to intersect the RMS body of the signal, maintaining consistent average level across the performance [GA]
**Sidechain:** N/A — no SC-HPF required; full-spectrum detection preserves natural tonal balance of the vocal [GA]
**Engineering Objective:** The FET stage attenuates erratic peak transients, preventing gain reduction artifacts in the Opto stage; the Opto stage provides smooth program-dependent leveling that maintains consistent RMS level regardless of the singer's proximity to the microphone. Net result: a high-RMS, low-crest-factor vocal signal with minimal audible gain reduction artifacts. [GA]

---

### Hyperpop

**Circuit:** Digital VCA, feed-forward with look-ahead [CB]
**Detection Method:** Peak, feed-forward [CB]
**Attack:** 0ms (look-ahead pre-reads incoming transients) [CB]
**Release:** 1ms–5ms [GA]
**Ratio:** ∞:1 (hard limiting / brick wall) [GA]
**Threshold:** Set to intersect the peak ceiling of the signal, allowing zero peak overshoot above the threshold; all signal energy above threshold is reduced to unity at the threshold level [GA]
**Sidechain:** N/A [GA]
**Engineering Objective:** Reduce crest factor to near-zero, producing a waveform approximating a rectangular pulse shape. With a 0ms attack and 1ms–5ms release, the gain reduction circuit re-triggers within the period of mid-frequency content (e.g., 1ms = one cycle of 1kHz), introducing amplitude modulation artifacts that manifest as inter-modulation distortion and a gritty, artificial tonal texture. This distortion is a primary sonic characteristic of the hyperpop vocal aesthetic, not a side effect to be minimised. [GA]

---

### Folk

**Circuit:** Opto or Vari-Mu [CB]
**Detection Method:** Program-dependent [CB]
**Attack:** 30ms–80ms [GA]
**Release:** Program-dependent; 100ms–500ms, slow tail [CB, GA]
**Ratio:** 1.5:1–2:1 [GA]
**Threshold:** Set above the average RMS level of the performance, targeting only the uppermost dynamic peaks; maximum 2dB–4dB of gain reduction on the loudest phrases [GA]
**Sidechain:** N/A [GA]
**Engineering Objective:** Reduce crest factor minimally while preserving the natural transient envelope of the performance and the decay tail of any room ambiance. The slow attack of the Opto or Vari-Mu circuit ensures the initial transient energy of consonants and picked string attacks passes unaffected, maintaining the natural "breath" and micro-dynamic variation of the performance. [GA]

---

### Traditional

**Circuit:** Vari-Mu or transparent Digital (look-ahead, low ratio) [CB]
**Detection Method:** Feed-back (Vari-Mu); feed-forward (Digital) [CB]
**Attack:** 50ms+ [GA]
**Release:** Auto / program-dependent; 200ms–1s [CB, GA]
**Ratio:** 1.5:1–2:1 per stage; serial double compression recommended for macro-dynamic management [GA]
**Threshold:** Set well above RMS floor, targeting only the largest dynamic excursions; 1dB–2dB of gain reduction per stage maximum [GA]
**Sidechain:** N/A [GA]
**Engineering Objective:** Manage macro-dynamic variations (e.g., verse-to-chorus level jumps) without introducing gain reduction artifacts into the sustain or decay portion of phrases. The very slow attack ensures the onset of each phrase is preserved at its natural amplitude. The program-dependent release prevents the gain reduction stage from audibly "letting go" between phrases, maintaining a consistent gain floor. [GA]

### Choir (Choral)

**Circuit:** Vari-Mu or transparent Digital [CB]
**Detection Method:** Feed-back (Vari-Mu); RMS feed-forward (Digital) [CB]
**Attack:** 50ms–100ms [GA]
**Release:** Auto / program-dependent; 300ms–1.5s [CB, GA]
**Ratio:** 1.5:1 per stage; serial compression across multiple stages to accumulate modest gain reduction without a single stage imposing an audible signature [GA]
**Threshold:** Set to intersect the ensemble's collective dynamic ceiling — the point at which the cumulative RMS of all voices in combination exceeds the target operating level; individual voice peaks below this ceiling pass unaffected [GA]
**Sidechain:** N/A — no HPF required; full-spectrum detection is appropriate for ensemble material [GA]
**Engineering Objective:** Control the cumulative dynamic build-up of a large ensemble (e.g., a fortissimo climax) without imposing audible ducking, suppression of reverberant field decay, or "sucking" of room ambiance. The very slow attack and long program-dependent release ensure that the gain reduction engages and releases at a rate below the threshold of auditory detection for these time constants. [GA]

### K-pop

**Circuit:** VCA, feed-forward [CB]
**Detection Method:** Peak, feed-forward [CB]
**Attack:** 8ms–12ms (preserves consonant transients above the attack envelope) [GA]
**Release:** 40ms–60ms [GA]
**Ratio:** 4:1–8:1 [GA]
**Threshold:** Set to intersect the upper RMS ceiling of the processed vocal body, allowing only the consonant attack transients (which exceed the RMS ceiling briefly) to pass unaffected through the attack window [GA]
**Sidechain:** SC-HPF at 80Hz–100Hz to prevent low-frequency proximity effect from triggering gain reduction; de-esser in series upstream to manage sibilant peak detection separately [GA]
**Engineering Objective:** Achieve maximum RMS density and spectral brightness while preserving consonant intelligibility for placement in dense, high-frequency arrangements. The VCA feed-forward topology provides precise and consistent gain reduction that does not vary with signal history, ensuring the vocal level remains tightly controlled across the full dynamic range of the performance. [GA]

### J-pop

**Circuit:** VCA, feed-forward [CB]
**Detection Method:** Peak, feed-forward [CB]
**Attack:** 10ms–15ms [GA]
**Release:** 60ms–80ms [GA]
**Ratio:** 3:1–4:1 [GA]
**Threshold:** Set to intersect the upper RMS ceiling of the vocal body; slightly higher threshold than K-pop to allow more natural dynamic variation in the signal body [GA]
**Sidechain:** SC-HPF at 80Hz; de-esser in series upstream [GA]
**Engineering Objective:** Controlled, mid-forward vocal presence with preserved consonant articulation and a slightly lower RMS ceiling than K-pop, retaining more natural vocal body weight. The lower ratio relative to K-pop allows the dynamic contour of the phrase to remain audible, supporting the melodic emphasis characteristic of J-pop vocal production. [GA]

### City Pop

**Circuit:** VCA (DBX-style, feed-forward) or Opto (program-dependent) [CB, GA]
**Detection Method:** Peak, feed-forward (VCA); Program-dependent (Opto) [CB]
**Attack:** 15ms–25ms [GA]
**Release:** 80ms–120ms [GA]
**Ratio:** 3:1–4:1 [GA]
**Threshold:** Set to intersect the average RMS level of the sustained vocal body, targeting the body of the phrase rather than the peak transients; allows the crest factor of the signal to remain partially intact [GA]
**Sidechain:** N/A — vocal sits in a mid-range dominated arrangement without significant low-frequency competition [GA]
**Engineering Objective:** Smooth, consistent RMS level with natural-feeling gain reduction that complements syncopated rhythmic phrasing. The moderate attack time allows the onset of each phrase to pass with its natural transient shape before gain reduction engages, preserving the articulation of the vocal over the rhythmically active instrumentation. [GA]

### Metal

**Circuit:** FET (1176-style) [CB]
**Detection Method:** Peak, feed-back [CB]
**Attack:** 20μs–200μs (fast; clamps peaks before transient propagates) [CB, GA]
**Release:** 50ms–100ms [GA]
**Ratio:** 8:1–20:1; "All Buttons In" mode (12:1–20:1 with plateau compression curve and elevated THD) applicable for extreme gain reduction and deliberate distortion [CB, GA]
**Threshold:** Set to target peak excursions above the RMS floor; 10dB+ of gain reduction on peaks is typical. Input drive on hardware implementations increases THD proportionally [CB, GA]
**Sidechain:** N/A — full-spectrum detection; no HPF required [GA]
**Engineering Objective:** The fast FET attack attenuates peak transients and increases the RMS-to-peak ratio. The transformer coupling and FET non-linearity under drive introduce even-order harmonic distortion, adding spectral density to the signal that increases its perceived energy and ability to cut through a high-SPL, distorted guitar arrangement. Elevated THD at "All Buttons In" settings produces a compressed-distortion artifact that increases vocal aggression. [CB, GA]

### Jazz

**Circuit:** Vari-Mu or Opto [CB]
**Detection Method:** Feed-back, program-dependent [CB]
**Attack:** 50ms+ [GA]
**Release:** Program-dependent; 300ms–1s [CB, GA]
**Ratio:** 1.5:1–2:1 [GA]

**Sidechain:** N/A [GA]

The Vari-Mu's soft-knee program-dependent ratio means compression begins imperceptibly and increases gradually, making the threshold crossing inaudible. [CB, GA]

### House (incl. Prog House)

**Circuit:** VCA, feed-forward [CB]
**Detection Method:** Peak, feed-forward [CB]
**Attack:** 5ms–15ms [GA]
**Release:** Timed to tempo; at 128 BPM (one beat = 468ms), release set to 200ms–350ms to return to unity gain before the next downbeat transient [GA]
**Ratio:** 6:1–8:1 [GA]
**Threshold:** Set to intersect the RMS ceiling of the vocal body, producing consistent gain reduction across the sustained phrase [GA]
**Sidechain:** SC-HPF at 100Hz–150Hz to isolate the sidechain detection circuit from kick drum low-frequency energy, preventing the kick from triggering gain reduction on the vocal [GA]
**Engineering Objective:** Maintain vocal presence above kick and bass energy without low-frequency modulation artifacts. SC-HPF prevents the kick drum's low-frequency content from triggering gain reduction cycles that would cause the vocal to duck in synchrony with the kick. BPM-timed release ensures the gain reduction envelope returns to unity gain in rhythm with the track, converting any residual pumping into a controlled, tempo-synchronous effect. [CB, GA]

### Choir (Choral)

**Circuit:** Vari-Mu [CB]
**Detection Method:** Feed-back, RMS-like [CB]
**Attack:** 80ms–150ms [GA]
**Release:** Program-dependent; 500ms–2s [CB, GA]

## Techniques

### Bus Processing

**Signal Architecture:** A compressor inserted on a stereo group bus receiving the summed output of a group of related instruments (e.g., all drums, all strings, all backing vocals). The compressor processes the summed stereo signal rather than individual channels. [CB]
**Recommended Circuit:** VCA (feed-back topology preferred for musical response) or Vari-Mu. Low ratios (1.5:1–2:1) are standard. [CB, GA]
**Time Constants:** Slow attack (20ms–50ms) to preserve the transient definition of the dominant instrument in the group; release timed to tempo or set to program-dependent to avoid rhythmic artifacts. [GA]
**Interaction With Genre Sections:** Applicable across all genre sections. Pop, K-pop, and J-pop drum sections benefit from VCA bus compression for RMS density; Folk, Jazz, and Blues instrument sections benefit from Vari-Mu bus processing for transparent group cohesion. House instrument and drum sections use bus compression as the architecture for kick-sidechain pumping. [GA]

---

### Peak Catching

**Signal Architecture:** A fast-acting compressor inserted in series before a primary leveling compressor (or limiter). Its function is limited to attenuating instantaneous peak transients that would otherwise cause the downstream stage to over-compress the signal body. [CB]
**Recommended Circuit:** FET or Digital VCA, feed-forward. The circuit must respond faster than the primary leveling stage. [CB]
**Time Constants:** Attack: 20μs–200μs (FET) or 0ms (Digital). Release: 50ms–100ms. Ratio: 4:1–8:1. Only 2dB–4dB of gain reduction is typically applied; peak catching does not level the signal body, it only attenuates the highest transient excursions. [CB, GA]
**Interaction With Genre Sections:** Directly implemented in the Pop Vocal serial FET+Opto configuration, where the FET stage performs peak catching before the Opto leveling stage. Applicable as a pre-stage in Metal and Rock vocal sections where extreme transient peaks would otherwise cause audible artifacts in the primary compressor. [GA]

---

### Glue Compression

**Signal Architecture:** A compressor applied to a stereo group bus or mix bus at low ratio and slow time constants with the objective of reducing the amplitude differential between individual elements of the group, making them appear to occupy a more cohesive spectral and dynamic space. [CB, GA]
**Recommended Circuit:** VCA (SSL G-Bus style, feed-back) or Vari-Mu. The feed-back VCA topology is specifically suited because the sidechain reacts to an already-compressed signal, producing a self-limiting response that resists over-compression. [CB]
**Time Constants:** Attack: 10ms–30ms; Release: 100ms–400ms or auto. Ratio: 1.5:1–2:1. Gain reduction: 1dB–3dB. [GA]
**Interaction With Genre Sections:** Most applicable to Pop, K-pop, J-pop, and City Pop instrument and drum bus sections where multiple simultaneously playing instruments require cohesion. Also applicable to the Choir (Choral) vocal section as a final stage after individual channel compression. [GA]

---

### NY Style Compression

**Signal Architecture:** A parallel compression architecture (see below) characterised by extreme settings on the compressed path: high ratio (8:1–20:1 or limiting), fast attack, fast release, producing a heavily saturated and distorted signal with near-zero crest factor. This compressed path is blended at low level (typically 10%–30% wet) beneath the dry signal. [GA]
**Recommended Circuit:** FET or Digital VCA on the compressed path. The FET circuit contributes even-order harmonic distortion that adds body to the blended signal beyond what a clean VCA provides. [CB, GA]
**Time Constants:** Compressed path: Attack 200μs–2ms; Release 20ms–80ms; Ratio 8:1–∞:1. The extreme settings are intentional — the compressed path is not intended to sound natural in isolation. [GA]
**Interaction With Genre Sections:** Most applicable to Metal and Rock drum sections (parallel FET on room mics or drum bus) and Hyperpop drum sections. Also referenced in Pop and Rock vocal sections where the NY parallel technique is used to add density without reducing the dynamic range of the primary vocal signal. [GA]

---

### Parallel Compression

**Signal Architecture:** The input signal is split into two paths: a dry (unprocessed) path and a wet (compressed) path. Both paths are recombined at the output at a set blend ratio. The dry path preserves the original peak transients and crest factor; the wet path provides a compressed, higher-RMS version of the signal. The recombined signal has a higher RMS floor (contributed by the wet path) while retaining the original peak transients (contributed by the dry path), resulting in a net increase in perceived density without the transient obliteration that would result from heavy serial compression. [CB, GA]
**Recommended Circuit:** VCA (clean, for density without coloration) or FET (for additional harmonic content on the wet path). [CB]
**Time Constants:** Wet path: Attack 1ms–10ms; Release 50ms–150ms; Ratio 4:1–8:1. The wet path time constants are set moderately — extreme settings are the domain of NY style compression. [GA]
**Interaction With Genre Sections:** Applicable to Pop, Rock, Metal, and House drum sections. Directly referenced in Hyperpop drum sections where the parallel FET path produces deliberate distortion. In the House instrument section, parallel compression with an external sidechain on the wet path produces the kick-triggered pumping effect while preserving transient integrity on the dry path. [GA]

## Pitfalls

### Transient Obliteration

**Pitfall:** Setting attack time below 5ms on percussive material or vocal consonants.
**Technical Cause:** The gain reduction circuit engages before the transient peak has fully propagated through the circuit. The peak of the transient — which defines the "click" of a kick, the "crack" of a snare, or the consonant onset of a vocal syllable — is attenuated proportionally to the ratio before it reaches the output stage. [CB]
**Signal-Domain Effect:** Reduction of the crest factor of the signal at the transient onset. The peak-to-RMS ratio of the affected transient is reduced, resulting in a signal that appears to have a slower attack envelope than the source material. This is perceived as the sound being "pushed back," "dulled," or "muffled" in the context of a mix. Consonant intelligibility is reduced on vocal material. [CB]
**Conditions:** Attack times below 5ms on any signal with a crest factor above 12dB (i.e., any signal with prominent transient content). Particularly severe on kick drum direct microphone signals, snare top signals, and unprocessed vocal recordings with prominent plosive and sibilant consonants. [GA]

---

### Harmonic Distortion from Short Release on Low-Frequency Content

**Pitfall:** Setting release time shorter than the period of the lowest frequency component in the signal.
**Technical Cause:** When the release time is shorter than the period of a low-frequency waveform cycle, the gain reduction envelope completes a full engagement-and-release cycle within a single waveform cycle. For a 50Hz signal (period = 20ms), any release time below 20ms will cause the compressor to re-trigger within the cycle. For a 100Hz signal (period = 10ms), any release below 10ms creates this condition. The gain reduction envelope effectively tracks the individual cycles of the waveform rather than the overall dynamic envelope. [CB]
**Signal-Domain Effect:** Amplitude modulation of individual waveform cycles at the frequency of the signal itself. This intra-cycle amplitude modulation produces harmonic distortion, manifesting as a "buzzing," "chattering," or "dirty" artifact on bass and low-frequency content. The distortion products appear at integer multiples of the fundamental frequency of the signal. [CB]
**Conditions:** Release times below 20ms on signals with fundamental frequencies below 50Hz; below 10ms on signals with fundamentals below 100Hz. Most commonly encountered when applying aggressive compression to electric bass, sub-bass synthesizer, and kick drum signals. In hyperpop production, this distortion is an intentional outcome; in all other genres, it is a defect. [CB, GA]

---

### Stereo Image Collapse

**Pitfall:** Over-compressing a stereo bus with linked detection at high ratios or fast time constants.
**Technical Cause:** In a stereo-linked compressor, the sidechain detection circuit sums or averages the level of both channels to derive a single control voltage applied identically to both channels. If a loud transient occurs on one channel only (e.g., a panned percussion hit on the far left), the sidechain generates a control voltage proportional to that transient's amplitude and applies equivalent gain reduction to the right channel, which contains no corresponding signal. [CB]
**Signal-Domain Effect:** The right channel is attenuated in proportion to an event on the left channel. This creates a correlated reduction in the amplitude differential between the two channels at the moment of the transient, reducing the stereo width. At extreme settings (high ratio, fast attack, high gain reduction), the stereo image collapses toward the centre as the dominant channel's transient causes both channels to reach similar amplitude. [CB]
**Conditions:** Stereo-linked compressors with ratios above 4:1, attack times below 10ms, and gain reduction exceeding 6dB on individual transients. Most commonly encountered on stereo mix buses and stereo drum overheads with hard-panned material. [CB, GA]

---

### Low-End Sidechain Modulation (Kick-Pumping)

**Pitfall:** Applying compression to a full-mix bus or group bus without a Sidechain High Pass Filter (SC-HPF) when the signal contains prominent low-frequency transients (kick drum).
**Technical Cause:** Without an SC-HPF, the sidechain detection circuit responds to the full frequency spectrum of the input signal, including the sub-bass and low-bass energy of the kick drum. The high amplitude of the kick's low-frequency content causes the sidechain to generate a large control voltage at each kick transient, triggering heavy gain reduction across the entire bus at the rate of the kick drum. [CB]
**Signal-Domain Effect:** The entire bus — including mid-range and high-frequency elements that are not dynamically related to the kick drum — is gain-reduced in synchrony with each kick hit. The result is an amplitude modulation of the full mix at the kick drum frequency, perceived as a rhythmic "ducking" or "pumping" of the overall level. Unlike the intentional House pumping effect (which is controlled and BPM-timed), this artifact occurs at the transient frequency of the kick including sub-bass harmonics, producing an irregular and tonally destructive modulation of the mix. [CB, GA]
**Conditions:** Any bus compressor applied to a mix or group containing a kick drum, without an SC-HPF set at 80Hz–150Hz. Most commonly encountered on mix bus compressors, drum bus compressors, and vocal bus compressors in arrangements where the kick occupies significant low-frequency amplitude. [CB, GA]

---

### Over-Compression of Reverberant Tail

**Pitfall:** Applying fast-release compression on room microphones or stereo bus in acoustically live recordings.
**Technical Cause:** When the compressor's release time is shorter than the RT60 of the recording space, the gain reduction envelope returns to unity gain before the reverberant decay of the room has resolved. On the next transient event, the compressor re-engages, attenuating the early reflections and diffuse reverberant field that follow each hit. [CB]
**Signal-Domain Effect:** The reverberant tail of each event is amplitude-modulated by the compressor's gain reduction cycle. The room ambiance appears to "duck" or "breathe" after each hit rather than decaying naturally. In ensemble recordings (Choir, Traditional, Jazz), this artifact destroys the natural acoustic space of the recording. [CB]
**Conditions:** Release times shorter than the RT60 of the recording space, applied to room microphones or stereo overhead/ambiance microphones in live room recordings. Common in Choir, Traditional, Jazz, and Blues contexts where the natural room decay is a primary component of the recording. [CB, GA]
